1. Unified Architecture Overview

1.1 Purpose and Scope
The Unified Identity‑Addressing‑Overlay Architecture defines a modern enterprise networking model where identity, addressing, and application context are the primary determinants of routing, segmentation, and policy enforcement. This architecture replaces legacy, topology‑bound constructs with an intent‑driven, application‑aware control plane that operates consistently across data center, Wide Area Network (WAN), cloud, and remote environments.

Scope includes:
- Identity‑centric routing and segmentation driven by authoritative identity services.
- Application‑aware overlays (Software‑Defined Networking (SDN); Software‑Defined Wide Area Networking (SD‑WAN)).
- Authoritative addressing and naming via IP Address Management (IPAM) and Domain Name System (DNS).
- Identity and trust services via Identity and Access Management (IAM), certificate and token services.
- Deterministic pathing using Segment Routing Traffic Engineering (SR‑TE) and Multiprotocol Label Switching Traffic Engineering (MPLS‑TE).
- Zero Trust and Secure Access Service Edge (SASE) alignment.
- Closed‑loop automation, telemetry, and observability.
- High availability and redundancy patterns for control, overlay, and underlay.

This section establishes the architecture’s intent, the operational boundaries, and the high‑level interactions between authorities, control, overlay, and underlay.

1.2 Principles and Design Tenets
The architecture is guided by the following tenets:
- Identity is the new perimeter. All access and forwarding decisions are anchored to verifiable identities (user, device, workload, application, non‑person entity).
- Addressing is authoritative. IPAM and DNS provide deterministic naming and addressing that map to application identity and policy.
- Overlay elevation. Logical forwarding and policy enforcement are implemented in the overlay so that the underlay is treated as a commodity transport.
- Intent over configuration. Business and security intent are expressed in a Policy Engine and translated into deterministic control‑plane actions.
- Application as the primary object. Applications (not IPs or VLANs) are the unit of policy, telemetry, and SLA enforcement.
- Determinism and resilience. Paths and failover behaviors are explicit, pre‑computed, and SLA‑aware.
- Continuous verification. Telemetry, identity events, and behavioral analytics drive automated remediation and drift reconciliation.
- Privacy and lawful use. Location and telemetry data are treated as potentially sensitive; collection and sharing must meet privacy, retention, and access control requirements.

These tenets ensure the architecture is scalable, auditable, and operationally deterministic.

1.3 Conceptual Model: Underlay, Overlay, and Authority
The architecture separates responsibilities into three interacting domains:
- Authority Plane (sources of truth): IP Address Management (IPAM); Identity and Access Management (IAM); Certificate and Token Services; Policy Engine. These systems define who and what, and express intent.
- Control Plane (translation and orchestration): Consumes authority inputs and translates intent into routing, segmentation, QoS, and enforcement rules across overlay and underlay. Includes intent translation, path orchestration, and closed‑loop automation.
- Data Plane (overlay + underlay):
  - Overlay Fabric: SDN in data centers and SD‑WAN across WAN/edge enforce identity‑based segmentation, application classification, and service insertion. Encapsulation examples include VXLAN, GRE, and IPsec.
  - Physical Underlay: MPLS, Dedicated Internet Access (DIA), LTE/5G, and campus L2/L3 provide deterministic transport primitives and QoS enforcement (SR‑TE, MPLS‑TE).

Operational separation: the overlay enforces policy and identity; the underlay guarantees transport characteristics. The control plane binds the two deterministically.

1.4 Operational Flow and Feedback Loop
Operational flow (practical sequence):
1. Telemetry collection: Underlay and overlay nodes, endpoints, and identity systems emit metrics (latency, jitter, loss), flow records, application classification, and location signals.
2. Correlation and decisioning: The Control Plane and Policy Engine correlate telemetry with identity and intent to select paths and enforcement actions.
3. Path binding and enforcement: Flows are bound to deterministic underlay paths (SR‑TE/MPLS‑TE) or overlay tunnels; enforcement points (SDN/SD‑WAN gateways, SASE nodes) apply segmentation and inspection.
4. Continuous feedback: Telemetry and identity events feed back into analytics and automation for drift detection, SLA remediation, and policy convergence.

This closed‑loop model ensures deterministic behavior, rapid remediation, and continuous alignment with intent.

1.5 Privacy, Compliance, and ATO Considerations
Privacy and lawful‑use constraints: Location and telemetry signals can contain personally identifiable information (PII). Agencies must perform Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs), define retention and access controls, and ensure telemetry sharing complies with applicable privacy and data residency policies before enabling location‑based optimizations.

Authority to Operate (ATO) guidance: FedRAMP authorization of cloud services reduces assessment burden but does not automatically grant an agency ATO. Agencies must use vendor FedRAMP artifacts (System Security Plan, Security Assessment Report, Plan of Actions & Milestones) and document any residual risks and compensating controls in their ATO packages.
